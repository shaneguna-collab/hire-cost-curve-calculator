When does a hire stop paying for itself?

A clinician's wage climbs every year — they move up the award bands and the new rates phase in. To hold your margin, their billings have to climb just as fast. This shows the billings they need each year, and the year that number outruns what a physio can realistically generate.

The wage climbs on autopilot. The billings don't.

Every year the clinician moves up the experience bands and the award rates phase in, so the wage rises whether or not anything else changes. To keep paying them the same share of their billings — your margin — they have to generate proportionally more. The billings-needed line is that target, rising in lockstep with the wage.

The problem is the year the target outruns reality

There's a ceiling on what one physio can bill in a week of work. When the billings they'd need to hold your margin climbs past that ceiling, the maths stops working — you either accept a thinner margin or you can't afford them at that share. That crossing year is when the squeeze actually arrives.

What is locked, and what is your call

The wage base is the published 1 October 2026 rate with the confirmed 4.75% review applied; 2028–2030 steps are interpolated between the Commission's two published figures; anything past 4.75% is your inflation assumption. Wage figures are base plus 12% super. Your share, the ceiling and inflation are yours to set.
